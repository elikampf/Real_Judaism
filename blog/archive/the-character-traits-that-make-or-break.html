The Character Traits That Make a Jewish Marriage

Learn the essential middos for Jewish marriage success, including humility, positivity, and anger management‚Äîcharacter traits that create lasting shalom bayis.

Share:

The Vilna Gaon said that the main purpose of being born into this world is to improve one's middos. This is perhaps the hardest work anyone will do in their lifetime.

In our quest to support ourselves and raise families, it's easy to get lost in everyday details and forget the ultimate goal. We need constant reminders about our real goals to be successful.

The best place to work on middos‚Äîand the most constant opportunity‚Äîis in marriage.

The Newlywed Reality Check

It's common for newlyweds to enter marriage with the best intentions to be the perfect spouse. "As soon as I get married, I'm quitting all those bad habits." That inspiration is a great start, but usually there's no real plan backing it up.

After a few months, when excitement has worn off and they've settled into reality, all those habits you were sure were history begin seeping back‚Äîoften to the new spouse's disappointment.

Marriage: Your Character Laboratory

Marriage is now your reality. Your job is creating your home, your relationship. This takes work‚Äîit doesn't happen automatically.

Marriage is the best medium to become the person you're supposed to be. Specifically because you're living with the one meant to complete you, you have opportunities to become that person. You were given each other for this purpose‚Äîthrough helping each other improve, you reach achdus.

This isn't easy. We all have challenges and weak points, and no one knows them better than your spouse. But that's exactly why marriage can bring out your best. When you're dedicated to building your home by bringing Hashem and love into your relationship, even the hardest work can be accomplished.

The Central Middah: Humility

Although every middah has significance, the central trait needed for true success is anavah‚Äîhumility.

Being humble doesn't mean allowing the other to walk all over you. It doesn't mean you're a shmatta. It means understanding your relationship and your role in it.

When Hashem created man, only together with a wife would there be one complete unit. Without a wife, a man is MISSING part of himself.

Our sages teach (Kiddushin 2b) that it's the way of a man to search for a wife like someone looking for his lost object. He needs her because she's part of him.

Obviously, the reverse is equally true. It's etched in every woman's genetics to want to connect to her husband‚Äîthe Torah tells us this explicitly.

Two Halves of One Whole

The relationship between husband and wife is the connection of two parts that, together, create one complete unit. This is Hashem's design and desire‚Äîfor couples to reach completion.

What's your role? Each spouse must be an anav. You must humbly recognize that you're only half of what you should become. You need your other half‚Äîwithout them, you'd be severely lacking.

Realize: I'm not independent, and I can't be independent if I ever want to reach my potential.

The central point is humbly accepting that you're lacking and need your spouse to become whole.

The Power of Positivity

Here's where this humility becomes practical. When you recognize your dependence on your spouse, you stop judging their shortcomings and start focusing on how to help them succeed.

What if you're doing your part but your spouse isn't doing theirs? This can be very frustrating. It's understandable when someone lashes out: "Why doesn't he show appreciation for everything I do?" "Why doesn't she see all the effort I'm putting in and respond?"

The Solution: Building Up, Not Tearing Down

The answer lies in realizing that besides doing your part, you can build up your spouse. Anger comes when you feel helpless, not receiving what you feel your spouse should provide. Hope and confidence come when you realize you have tools to strengthen your spouse.

Hashem specifically put you together because you're the perfect fit to help each other reach potential. The key isn't blaming the other for shortcomings or expressing anger. Rather, Hashem is showing you exactly where your job is to positively encourage and help your spouse.

Why Positivity Works

Positivity is crucial. Someone who feels good about themselves accomplishes more. When you feel loved and respected for good things you do and your inherent potential, it's much easier to fulfill that potential.

This is the path every couple should use to build each other up, for each spouse to reach their potential and together build a bayis ne'eman b'Yisrael.

The Most Destructive Force: Anger

The most destructive force in marriage is anger. When there's kaas in the home, the Shechinah doesn't want to be there.

The pasuk says: "Lo teva'aru eish b'chol moshvoseichem b'yom haShabbos." The Zohar HaKadosh explains that the "fire" refers to the fire of anger. The Torah warns us that the special neshama yeseira‚Äîthe extra bit of holy soul infused on Shabbos‚Äîis very sensitive, allergic to anger. If there's anger, that holy soul leaves!

Therefore, the Torah warns: "Don't light a fire of anger in your homes on Shabbos."

The Divine Presence in Marriage

The same is true about the relationship between husband and wife. Hashem wants to put Himself in our homes. He wants to dwell among us.

The letter "yud" from the word Ish (man) and the letter "hey" from the word Isha (woman) represent how Hashem puts Himself into a relationship where husband and wife truly love each other.

But when there's anger in the home, the Shechinah leaves. This creates a downward spiral‚Äînow there's no longer "yud" or "hey," and the ish and isha are left only as aish and aish‚Äîfire and more fire, with more destruction.

The Antidote to Anger

It doesn't need to be this way. Remind yourself who you are and what you're here to do. My spouse is part of me‚ÄîI'm not whole without him/her.

Changing your perspective of your relationship will also change your perspective of what's causing current anger. If I would have been the cause of this incident, how would I wish my spouse to react to me?

This is actually the truth, because your spouse is you, so your reaction should express that.

When you accustom yourself to viewing your spouse this way, your life will dramatically change for the better. You'll bring Hashem back into your home.

The Daily Practice

Here's a specific daily practice for implementing these middos:

Every single day, take time to write down and reflect on this idea: "My wife/husband is part of me."

The more you do this exercise, the more this idea becomes implanted deeply in your heart. Your love and desire to give to your spouse will become much stronger and more natural.

No Perfect People

Remember: until Mashiach arrives, no individual can possibly be perfect‚Äîdevoid of all flaws. It's important for both husband and wife to realize this.

By and large, strife and arguments leading to lack of shalom bayis require active participation from both parties. When one party realizes the other isn't taking the conflict to heart‚Äîneither being emotionally pulled in nor participating in arguments‚Äîlittle by little, fierce emotions and desire for fighting will ebb and ultimately fade away.

The Ultimate Goal

Marriage is your opportunity to become the person Hashem created you to be. Every challenge is an opportunity for growth. Every difficulty with your spouse is a chance to practice the middos that will serve you for life.

When you approach marriage as your personal character development laboratory, with humility, positivity, and control over anger, you're not just building a better marriage‚Äîyou're building a better you.

And that better you will create the kind of home where Hashem wants to dwell.

Try it.
